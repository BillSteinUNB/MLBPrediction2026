Targeted Research for MLB Run-Count and Totals Prediction ModelingThe development of a highly profitable predictive model for Major League Baseball (MLB) run-count and totals forecasting requires a departure from traditional, generalized baseball analytics. To generate a quantifiable edge against highly efficient sports betting markets, the modeling pipeline must integrate granular, non-traditional features, accurately capture the extreme variance of run distributions, optimally blend algorithmic outputs with market priors, and evaluate performance using strict calibration metrics rather than binary accuracy. The following research exhaustively details the most promising vectors for material model improvement, focusing explicitly on actionable, implementation-ready findings.1. MLB Totals-Specific Feature IdeasTo outpace the market closing line, feature engineering must capture the physical, biomechanical, and human elements that dictate the run-scoring environment. The research strongly indicates that atmospheric physics, granular catcher framing metrics, bullpen leverage-performance data, and umpire tendencies provide the most substantial predictive lift.1.1 Atmospheric Physics and the Air Density Index (ADI)Claim: Air density—calculated as a composite of altitude, temperature, humidity, and barometric pressure—is a vastly superior predictor of batted ball distance and run-scoring environments than standalone temperature.
Source: Research on the physics of baseball flight, aerodynamic drag, and DFS weather modeling.
Confidence: Very High. The physical relationship between air density, aerodynamic drag, and the Magnus effect is mathematically proven.
Why it matters to our model: Traditional models often use temperature as a blunt proxy for weather. However, atmospheric physics dictates that humid air is actually less dense than dry air because water vapor molecules weigh less than the nitrogen and oxygen they replace. Furthermore, barometric pressure fluctuates independently of altitude, meaning high-pressure systems increase air density and suppress offense, while low-pressure systems decrease air density and boost offense. A 10% decrease in air density produces a 4% increase in the distance a batted ball travels, which radically alters the probability of extra-base hits and home runs. Thinner air reduces both drag forces (allowing the ball to travel further) and Magnus forces (reducing pitch movement), creating a compounded advantage for hitters.
Concrete implementation idea: Eradicate standalone temperature and elevation features. Instead, implement a calculated Air Density Index (ADI) that dynamically computes $\rho$ (air density) using stadium altitude, game-time temperature, relative humidity, and barometric pressure. Source: Empirical analysis of DFS player performance under varying weather conditions.
Confidence: High. The statistical deviation in strikeout-to-walk ratios during precipitation is distinct and measurable.
Why it matters to our model: Precipitation is frequently viewed by novice modelers as a negative for offensive output due to heavy air or potential delays. However, empirical data demonstrates that batters reach base 33.1% of the time in the rain compared to 32.0% in clear skies. More importantly, pitchers walk 9.6% more batters and strike out 10.1% fewer batters in rainy conditions, resulting in a 3.6% overall increase in run scoring. Wet baseballs reduce spin efficiency, grip, and command, directly feeding the run environment.
Concrete implementation idea: Introduce a categorical feature for Precipitation_Active. Apply a dynamic modifier to the starting pitcher's projected BB% (Walk Rate) and K% (Strikeout Rate) when this feature is triggered, rather than applying a blanket adjustment to the game's run total. This ensures the model accounts for the compounding effect of extra baserunners.1.3 Catcher Framing in the "Shadow Zone"Claim: Catcher pitch-framing in the "Shadow Zone" (the edges of the strike zone) quantifiably shifts plate appearance (PA) strikeout probabilities and compounds significantly to affect full-game run totals.
Source: Statcast catcher framing leaderboards, PA-level strike probability modeling, and random forest framing analysis.
Confidence: Very High. Pitch-tracking data confirms that top-tier framing catchers can save between 15 and 25 runs per season.
Why it matters to our model: A catcher with a +2 framing runs metric (top 15% of regulars) shifts the per-PA strikeout probability by approximately 1.0 to 2.0 percentage points. This effect is asymmetrical; it is magnified when paired with high-strikeout pitchers who frequently throw to the borders of the strike zone. Over a full game, an elite framing catcher can increase a pitcher's expected strikeout total by 0.5 to 0.8 Ks, which directly suppresses run scoring and strongly correlates with games hitting the "Under". Most public projection systems fail to model framing at the PA level.
Concrete implementation idea: Scrape Statcast for "Shadow Strike %" and "Catcher Framing Runs". Create an interaction feature between Pitcher_Shadow_Zone_Frequency (how often the starter targets the edges) and Catcher_Shadow_Strike_Percent. Because the effect of framing is non-linear at the extremes of the count (e.g., 0-2 vs. 3-0), utilize Isotonic Regression to map this framing interaction term to the expected strikeout rate.1.4 Bullpen Leverage-Performance (LevP) and Immediate FatigueClaim: Aggregate bullpen ERA is a highly flawed metric for totals prediction; modeling a bullpen's specific High-Leverage Performance (LevP) alongside rolling 3-day pitch counts is highly predictive of late-inning run scoring.
Source: Statistical analyses of MLB bullpen management, the Leverage Index (LI), and bullpen fatigue modeling.
Confidence: High. Modern MLB managerial tactics strictly reserve the best relief arms for high-leverage situations.
Why it matters to our model: An "average" bullpen might possess two elite, high-strikeout arms and four highly volatile, low-strikeout arms. If the two elite arms have thrown more than 30 pitches in the last three days, they are functionally unavailable. The team will be forced to deploy lower-leverage arms in high-leverage situations, drastically increasing the probability of late-game runs. Leverage-performance (LevP) measures how well pitchers perform specifically when the Leverage Index (LI) is high (LI > 1.0). Evaluating a bullpen without filtering for immediate fatigue causes the model to overestimate a team's run prevention capabilities.
Concrete implementation idea: Abandon season-long aggregate Bullpen ERA. Calculate an Available_Bullpen_LevP feature. Identify the top three relievers by Leverage Index (LI) for each team. Cross-reference their pitch counts over the previous three days (PC L3). If PC_L3 > 30, exclude them from the available pool for that game. Calculate the expected late-inning runs based solely on the K% and BB% of the remaining, rested bullpen arms.1.5 Umpire Accuracy Profiles and Thermal DegradationClaim: Home plate umpires exhibit persistent, measurable differences in strike zone accuracy, and this accuracy degrades quantifiably in extreme temperatures, directly impacting run totals.
Source: Empirical umpire scorecard analyses and academic research on the impact of climate on MLB officiating.
Confidence: Medium-High.
Why it matters to our model: The average MLB umpire misses roughly 12 ball-strike calls per game, operating at an average accuracy of 85% to 92%. Umpires with tighter strike zones force more walks and longer counts, increasing the probability of "Overs". Conversely, umpires with wider zones suppress offense. Furthermore, research indicates a clear inverted U-pattern regarding temperature and umpire accuracy. Accuracy peaks between 80 and 90 degrees but falls by approximately 1 percentage point when temperatures exceed 95 degrees, particularly on borderline pitches in the shadow zone.
Concrete implementation idea: Incorporate an Umpire_Accuracy_Expected and Umpire_Runs_Favor metric based on trailing scorecard data. Create a secondary penalty feature that reduces umpire accuracy by 1% if the game-time temperature exceeds 95 degrees. This interaction should dynamically widen or narrow the expected walk rate (BB/9) for the starting pitchers in the simulation.1.6 Platoon Splits and the Regression of Hitter SkillClaim: Platoon splits (performance differences based on pitcher handedness) are vital for lineup evaluation, but raw historical splits are highly susceptible to variance and must be heavily regressed to the mean based on the hitter's handedness.
Source: Sabermetric research on estimating hitter platoon skills and "Cluster Luck".
Confidence: High. The stabilization thresholds for platoon splits are mathematically well-documented.
Why it matters to our model: Left-handed batters display significantly more variation in platoon skill than right-handed batters. Therefore, left-handed splits require less regression to the mean than right-handed splits. According to empirical studies, a left-handed hitter's platoon skill stabilizes against league average after approximately 1,000 plate appearances against left-handed pitchers, whereas a right-handed hitter requires 2,200 plate appearances to stabilize. Using unregressed platoon data for players below these thresholds will cause the model to overreact to small-sample splits, skewing the expected run total.
Concrete implementation idea: Apply Bayesian shrinkage to individual player platoon splits based on their career plate appearances against opposite-handed pitching. For lefties with fewer than 1,000 PAs and righties with fewer than 2,200 PAs against same-handed pitching, shrink their observed split toward the league-average split for their handedness.1.7 Travel Fatigue and Circadian MisalignmentClaim: Circadian rhythm disruptions and travel-induced fatigue following multi-time-zone travel significantly impact team performance and run-scoring capabilities.
Source: Sports analytics methodologies evaluating travel fatigue in professional baseball.
Confidence: Medium. While logical, the exact run-value impact is often secondary to fundamental pitching/hitting metrics.
Why it matters to our model: The grueling 162-game MLB schedule forces teams to cross multiple time zones with minimal rest. Sleep deprivation and circadian misalignment affect bat speed, reaction time, and pitcher velocity. Teams playing the first game of a series after crossing two or more time zones without a rest day operate at a measurable physiological disadvantage.
Concrete implementation idea: Create a categorical feature representing Time_Zones_Crossed_Last_24H. If the value is $\geq 2$, apply a minor fractional penalty to the team's expected wOBA and a minor inflation to their expected runs allowed.2. Market-Prior UsageIntegrating betting market priors—specifically the odds established by sharp bookmakers—into a predictive model is an intricate process. If executed poorly, it creates catastrophic data leakage. If executed optimally, it blends algorithmic historical forecasting with real-time, unquantifiable market intelligence (e.g., late scratches, unannounced injuries, and clubhouse dynamics).2.1 Bayesian Shrinkage Toward Sharp Closing LinesClaim: Predictive models optimized solely for absolute accuracy fail in betting markets because they correlate too highly with the bookmaker's margin; shrinking the model's prediction toward the sharp market closing line (e.g., Pinnacle) significantly improves long-term calibration and profitability.
Source: Research on exploiting sports betting markets, Bayesian forecast combinations, and Empirical Bayes methods.
Confidence: Very High. Pinnacle's closing lines exhibit an $R^2 = 0.997$ correlation with observed empirical probabilities over massive sample sizes.
Why it matters to our model: Pure machine learning models will occasionally output extreme predictions due to edge-case feature combinations (e.g., predicting 11.5 runs when the market total is 8.5). While the model perceives a mathematical edge, massive divergences almost always indicate missing qualitative variables (e.g., the manager resting three star players). Bayesian shrinkage acts as a vital regularization technique. The model's raw projection serves as the prior, and the market consensus serves as the likelihood. Pulling the final projection toward the market prevents disastrous over-exposure on incomplete data.
Concrete implementation idea: Implement an Empirical Bayes shrinkage formula during the bet-sizing phase. Calculate the variance of the model's historical predictions ($\sigma^2_{model}$) and the variance of the market line ($\sigma^2_{market}$). Use the interpolation factor $B = \frac{\sigma^2_{market}}{\sigma^2_{market} + \sigma^2_{model}}$ to compute the final, shrunk projection: Final_Proj = B * Market_Line + (1 - B) * Model_Proj. Size bets based on the delta between the Final_Proj and the market.2.2 Data Leakage from Opening and Closing LinesClaim: Training a machine learning model using historical closing odds as a predictive feature causes severe data leakage and overfitting, rendering the model incapable of identifying value against real-time odds.
Source: Algorithmic betting community analyses on feature representation and overfitting.
Confidence: Very High. This is a foundational pitfall in quantitative sports modeling.
Why it matters to our model: If historical closing lines are fed into the training matrix, the algorithm quickly learns that the closing line is a near-perfect predictor of the outcome. When deployed live, the model will only have access to the current (non-closing) line. The model will mistakenly treat the current, inefficient line with the same mathematical confidence it granted the highly efficient historical closing line, destroying its ability to find true discrepancies (edges).
Concrete implementation idea: Never include the market line (opening or closing) as a feature inside the core machine learning training matrix (e.g., XGBoost, Random Forest). The model must generate a "blind" prediction using only baseball fundamentals. Market lines must only be introduced in the post-processing phase to calculate expected value.2.3 Market Anchoring and OverreactionClaim: Sports betting markets exhibit anchoring bias to pre-season priors and frequently overreact to short-term, negatively autocorrelated sequences (momentum).
Source: Financial economics literature analyzing sports market efficiency and behavioral biases.
Confidence: Medium. While markets are highly efficient at closing, inefficiencies exist in the movement from the opener to the closer.
Why it matters to our model: Bettors and sportsbooks inherently anchor to outdated priors, such as a team's preseason win total or a pitcher's historical Cy Young status, even as current underlying metrics degrade. Furthermore, markets tend to overreact to recent extreme events (e.g., a team scoring 12 runs the previous night), exhibiting negatively autocorrelated changes that sophisticated models can exploit.
Concrete implementation idea: Create a Recency_Delta feature that measures the divergence between a team's performance over the last 5 games (L5) and their season-long moving average. If the market line shifts aggressively based on L5 variance, the "blind" fundamentals model will naturally fade the overreaction, identifying value on the contrarian side.3. Distribution ModelingEstimating the expected value (mean) of runs is vastly insufficient for a profitable betting model. Profitability requires mapping the precise probability distribution of outcomes across the integer spectrum to accurately price totals and their derivative markets.3.1 The Fallacy of the Poisson DistributionClaim: Baseball run-scoring fundamentally violates the core assumption of the Poisson distribution (that the mean equals the variance), suffering from severe overdispersion.
Source: Statistical modeling of MLB run frequencies and distribution mathematics.
Confidence: Very High. Supported by decades of empirical baseball data.
Why it matters to our model: The Poisson distribution assumes $E(X) = Var(X)$. However, in MLB, the variance is typically more than double the mean. For example, in the American League (2011-2013), the average runs per game per team was 4.49, but the variance was 9.99. Because the variance is so high, a Poisson model systematically under-predicts the likelihood of extreme events (shutouts and massive blowouts) and over-predicts the likelihood of scores grouping tightly around the mean. Relying on Poisson leads to highly miscalibrated probabilities for alternative totals (e.g., Over 11.5 or Under 5.5).
Concrete implementation idea: Eradicate all Poisson-based regression frameworks from the pipeline. Upgrade the algorithm to an overdispersed count framework, specifically the Negative Binomial (NB2) distribution. The NBD adds a specific dispersion parameter ($k$) to account for the heavy tails of run scoring, yielding drastically superior calibration.3.2 Mixture Models and Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB)Claim: Baseball run distributions require Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB) or finite mixture models because the probability of scoring zero runs operates via a distinct data-generating process compared to the count of runs once scoring begins.
Source: Advanced statistical research on discrete probability distributions, mixture modeling, and ecology data applications.
Confidence: High. Count modeling in sports routinely benefits from zero-inflation techniques.
Why it matters to our model: Approximately 73% of individual MLB half-innings are scoreless. A shutout is rarely random; it is often preserved by specific managerial decisions, such as deploying a high-leverage closer or implementing defensive shifts specifically to maintain a zero. Because zeros are generated by this secondary process, standard NBD models still slightly underestimate the shutout rate. A ZINB model utilizes a logistic regression to predict the probability of a zero, and simultaneously runs a Negative Binomial regression to predict the run count if the zero state is bypassed.
Concrete implementation idea: Transition the distribution modeling architecture to ZINB. The logistic portion of the ZINB model (predicting the zero) should heavily weight Bullpen_LevP and Starter_K_Percent (features that dictate run prevention and shutouts). The count portion (predicting the volume of runs) should heavily weight Park_Factor, Weather_ADI, and Batter_wOBA (features that dictate blowouts once runners are on base).3.3 The Extreme Variance and Flatness of Full-Game TotalsClaim: The distribution of full-game MLB totals is remarkably flat between 5 and 14 runs, exhibiting an enormous standard deviation (~4.58 runs) relative to the half-run increments set by bookmakers.
Source: Statcast data analysis of 2023 full-season empirical run distributions.
Confidence: High. Verified by raw empirical outcomes over thousands of games.
Why it matters to our model: A game with a posted total of 8.5 has almost an equal chance of landing on 5 runs (15.1% frequency), 7-9 runs (17.9%), or 11-14 runs (18.0%). Because the variance is so wide, predicting a mean of 9.2 against a market line of 8.5 provides a mathematical edge, but one that is easily swallowed by standard deviation and the bookmaker's vigorish (vig). A 0.7-run edge is a weak signal in a room full of noise.
Concrete implementation idea: Adjust the betting execution threshold dynamically based on the 4.58 standard deviation. The model must require a significantly larger delta between its projected mean and the market line (e.g., >1.5 runs) before triggering a bet. Training-Window and Regime ResearchThe integrity of historical training data is paramount. Using unadjusted data from 2018-2019 presents significant hazards due to fundamental regime shifts in MLB rules implemented prior to the 2023 season.4.1 The 2023 Shift Ban and BABIP AdjustmentsClaim: The 2023 ban on the defensive shift radically altered the run-scoring environment, particularly for left-handed pull hitters, rendering pre-2023 BABIP (Batting Average on Balls In Play) data highly inaccurate for modern projections.
Source: MLB halfway mark analysis and UCLA DataRes studies on rule changes.
Confidence: Very High. The statistical jump in BABIP is irrefutable.
Why it matters to our model: The rule requiring two infielders on each side of second base caused BABIP on pulled groundballs to increase by 19 points overall, and a massive 40 points for left-handed batters. A model trained equally on 2018-2022 data will systematically under-project hits and runs for lineups featuring left-handed pull hitters, resulting in inaccurate "Under" predictions and a miscalibrated run environment.
Concrete implementation idea: Apply an asymmetric decay weight to pre-2023 data. Do not use raw pre-2023 box score outcomes for training. Instead, isolate the historical batted-ball profiles (Launch Angle, Exit Velocity, Spray Angle) of players from 2018-2022, and recalculate their expected wOBA and expected BABIP using the 2023+ defensive positioning constraints.4.2 The Pitch Clock's Impact on Pitcher Fatigue and PaceClaim: The 2023 introduction of the pitch timer (15 seconds empty / 20 seconds on base) reduced game times by ~26 minutes, increased stolen base efficiency, and fractured traditional pitcher statistic clustering due to reduced physical recovery time between pitches.
Source: MLB game length analysis, biomechanical/fatigue research, and Statcast clustering data.
Confidence: High.
Why it matters to our model: Pitchers can no longer stall to recover stamina during high-leverage, runner-on-base situations. This has led to weakened correlations between ERA, strikeouts, and walks compared to pre-2023 baseline data. Furthermore, disengagement limits (two pickoff attempts) resulted in stolen base success rates jumping to over 80% (up from ~75%), moving runners into scoring position at a historically high rate and increasing run totals from 8.66 (2022) to 9.14 (2023).
Concrete implementation idea: The baseline run environment intercept in the model must be calibrated strictly to 2023 and 2024 data. Introduce a Runner_on_Base_Pace penalty feature for starting pitchers who historically relied on long intervals between pitches (utilizing time-between-pitches metrics from pre-2023), as they are most susceptible to fatigue and control degradation under the new clock.4.3 The 2020 COVID Season AnomalyClaim: The 2020 60-game season contains severe statistical anomalies that introduce noise into predictive models, justifying its complete exclusion or severe down-weighting.
Source: Umpire scorecard research and schedule variance analysis.
Confidence: High.
Why it matters to our model: The 2020 season featured regional-only schedules, no fans in the stands (eliminating traditional home-field crowd pressure on umpires), extreme anomalies in umpire incorrect calls (dropping to 4.72% compared to the usual ~12%), and a distorted bullpen usage environment. Including this data poisons the variance metrics and disrupts home-field advantage calculations.
Concrete implementation idea: Exclude the 2020 season from the training window entirely. The environmental differences create data artifacts that do not generalize to the current 162-game, full-attendance marathon structure.5. Betting Evaluation and ProfitabilityTo build a profitable model, the metrics used to evaluate the algorithm during backtesting must perfectly align with the mechanics and mathematics of sports betting. Standard data science metrics are highly misleading in this domain.5.1 Calibration Over Accuracy (CRPS and Brier Score)Claim: Evaluating betting models on binary Accuracy, ROC-AUC, or simple Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) is dangerously misleading; profitability is far more sensitive to Calibration Error, measured via the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) and Brier Score.
Source: AI in sports betting validation literature, machine learning economics, and calibration optimization studies.
Confidence: Very High. This is a consensus among quantitative sports syndicates.
Why it matters to our model: A model can rank outcomes perfectly (high AUC) and correctly identify the winner, but if it assigns a 65% probability to an event that only occurs 55% of the time, the model is severely miscalibrated. In betting, taking -110 odds (52.4% implied probability) requires exact calibration to size bets correctly via the Kelly Criterion. Overconfident models will recommend massive bet sizes on negative Expected Value (-EV) lines. CRPS evaluates the entire probability distribution (crucial for continuous run totals), penalizing predictions that place mass far away from the actual outcome.
Concrete implementation idea: Replace RMSE and Log-Loss with CRPS as the primary loss function or evaluation metric during the model's hyperparameter tuning phase. Apply Isotonic Regression post-prediction to force the model's outputs to map linearly to empirical observed frequencies, ensuring probabilities are perfectly recalibrated before bet sizing.5.2 Closing Line Value (CLV) vs. Expected ROI (xROI)Claim: While CLV is the gold standard for proving a long-term theoretical edge, utilizing Expected ROI (xROI) by "de-lucking" actual match results strips out extreme variance and stabilizes model evaluation much faster.
Source: Professional sports betting analytics frameworks (e.g., Unabated, Rufus Peabody).
Confidence: High.
Why it matters to our model: Betting an MLB game Under 8.5 that goes to the 9th inning tied 3-3, only to witness a 4-run 10th inning, results in a loss due to extra-inning variance. Traditional ROI metrics count this as a complete failure. xROI calculates the probability of the bet covering based on the score at the end of regulation (9 innings). It treats the 9-inning result as the "true" outcome and determines how lucky or unlucky the actual payout was.
Concrete implementation idea: Build an evaluation script that calculates xROI. Strip out extra-inning runs from the historical backtest. If betting an Under 8.5, and the score after 9 innings is 3-3 (Total 6), the model is credited with a high xROI, confirming the predictive logic was correct despite the binary financial loss. This prevents the discarding of valid predictive features due to bad variance.5.3 The Draw/Push Blindspot and Edge ThresholdingClaim: Machine learning models inherently avoid predicting outcomes with high variance (like pushes or exact number totals), compensating by inflating confidence in the most common outcomes, destroying profitability against the bookmaker's margin.
Source: Research on AI model behavior in sports betting and calibration.
Confidence: Medium-High.
Why it matters to our model: Because bookmakers charge a 4-5% vig, a model must not only be right, but right by a wide margin. If the model artificially inflates its confidence on "safe" totals just to reduce baseline error, it will trigger bets into heavily juiced, negative EV markets.
Concrete implementation idea: Establish a strict, dynamic edge threshold. Given the 4.58 standard deviation in MLB totals , the model should only output a bet recommendation if the calculated Expected Value (+EV) exceeds a minimum threshold (e.g., > 4.0% EV) after Vig is applied.6. Actionable Market SelectionNot all betting markets are priced with the same efficiency. Focusing the pipeline on derivative markets isolates predictive signals and avoids the noise of unmodelable late-game baseball mechanics.6.1 The Superiority of First Five (F5) TotalsClaim: First Five Innings (F5) totals are significantly more modelable and exhibit lower volatility than full-game totals because they isolate the starting pitcher and neutralize the high-variance elements of bullpen management and late-game pinch-hitting.
Source: Sports betting market efficiency analyses and F5 betting strategies.
Confidence: Very High.
Why it matters to our model: In the modern MLB era, starting pitchers rarely pitch deep into games. The final 3-4 innings are a parade of relievers. Handicapping full games requires compounding probabilities across 6-8 different pitchers, exponentially increasing the margin of error. F5 bets allow a model to leverage a highly accurate projection of a single Starting Pitcher vs. Starting Lineup matchup. F5 eliminates the chaos of the bullpen.
Concrete implementation idea: Pivot the primary focus of the predictive pipeline to F5 Totals. F5 markets are typically priced at halves (e.g., 4.5 or 5.5). The model's strength in analyzing starting pitcher wOBA allowed, shadow-zone framing, and top-of-the-order platoon splits will yield the highest ROI here, devoid of bullpen leakage.6.2 Asymmetric Bullpen Exploitation via Team TotalsClaim: Team Totals offer more profitable opportunities than Full Game Totals when there is a severe mismatch between one team's hot offense and the opposing team's fatigued bullpen.
Source: Algorithmic bullpen data betting strategies.
Confidence: High.
Why it matters to our model: If Team A has a tremendous offense facing Team B's overworked bullpen, but Team A's ace pitcher is on the mound, the Full Game Total might be suppressed (e.g., Under 7.5) due to Team A's ace. Betting the "Over" is risky because Team B won't score. Betting Team A's Team Total Over isolates the exact vulnerability (Team B's bullpen) without requiring the opposing team to contribute runs.
Concrete implementation idea: Generate isolated run distributions for the Away Team and Home Team separately, rather than just summing them for a game total. Calculate the delta between the model's Team Total projection and the sportsbook's Team Total line to exploit isolated mismatches.7.
